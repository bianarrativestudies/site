Welcome

Narrative, the form of meaning common to stories of all kinds, is a pervasive phenomenon in contemporary culture and a key element of the cultural history of civilisations worldwide. We think of it first, perhaps, in relation to fiction and literature, but fictional stories take many other forms, in media such as film and television, performance, dance, graphic narrative, interactive and digital media, oral storytelling, even dreams. And beyond fictional stories are the many forms of non-fictional narrative to be found in (for example) journalism, historiography, documentary, and autobiography, as well as in the social sciences and indeed the hard sciences, quite apart from our ordinary everyday reliance upon narrative in communicating and understanding personal experience. Indeed, the basic ways in which we comprehend experience in the form of stories are deeply ingrained in how we think - we, that is, as human beings: the foundations of narrative are to be found in distinctive features of human cognition.

Given the protean nature of the object of inquiry, Narrative Studies is necessarily an interdisciplinary and transmedia field in which theory, critical practice and empirical study are all in continual dialogue. The British and Irish Association for Narrative Studies exists to draw together these common interests across the institutional and disciplinary boundaries within academia, as well as between academic, creative and communicative approaches to narrative.
